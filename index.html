Youth of all races break the law. Youth of color are more likely to be punished for it.


It's #NotJusticeWhen the treatment is unequal.

The overrepresentation of youth in the juvenile justice system is called disproportionate minority contact (DMC for short).

DMC stems from bias. Researchers note that bias does not always happen on purpose. Nonetheless, it can sneak in whenever someone makes a decision.

System actors diagram

By way of example, among all races, about one in five youth use marijuana.




Chicago decriminalized marijuana in 2012. Illinois followed suit in 2016. As such, the number of arrests for marijuana possession has fallen.

Marijuana arrests over time

However, black kids consistently account for between 6 and 7 of every 10 arrests. They account for only 4 of every 10 kids in Chicago.

Marijuana arrests by race

In Chicago, black youth were arrested twice as often as white youth in 2014.

{% include district_map.html %}

DMC begins before youth even enter the juvenile justice system and persists as they move through it.


It's #NotJusticeWhen the deck is stacked.

Students who are suspended or expelled are 3 times more likely to make contact with the justice system than students who are not removed from school.

During the 2015-16 school year in Chicago Public Schools, black students made up:

  • 38.9% of Chicago Public Schools enrollment
  • 65.7% of school-based arrests
  • 70.7% of CPS out-of-school suspensions
  • 75.6% of CPS expulsions

DMC occurs at every point of contact in the justice system.

Points of contact diagram (extends system actors)

As a consequence, the majority of youth at every stage of the justice system is black.

DMC reaches beyond the justice system.


It's #NotJusticeWhen the consequences are permanent.

A criminal record makes it more difficult to get an education, find housing, and go to work.

Chicago Public Schools students who are arrested are more likely to drop out of high school and less likely to attend a four-year college then their peers.


SOURCE: Juvenile Arrest and Collateral Educational Damage in the Transition to Adulthood, a long-term study that tracked peer groups of CPS students across multiple years

58% of teens released from incarceration in Illinois return to prison within three years.

This is called recidivism, and it's a result of these obstacles combined with strict conditions of probation and parole.
