Youth of all races break the law. Youth of color are more likely to be punished.


This phenomenon is called disproportionate minority contact (DMC for short), and it stems from bias.

Researchers note that bias does not always happen on purpose. Nonetheless, it can affect who an officer arrests, when an attorney prosecutes, and how a judge sentences.

Youth from each race use marijuana at the same rate, about one in five.





Yet black youth were 1.6 times more likely than all youth to be arrested for it.

{% include expectation_v_reality_chart.html %}

This pattern is not just true of arrests overall – it's worse. In 2014, black youth in Chicago were 1.9 times more likely to be arrested than all youth in Chicago.

{% include arrest_v_population_chart.html %}
{% include district_map.html %}

The solution is not fewer arrests.

Thanks to decriminalization, the number of arrests for marijuana possession has fallen to virtually zero in 2017.

{% include arrest_chart.html %}

While possession was still an arrestable offense, however, the proportion of arrests happening to black kids stayed the same.

{% include race_chart.html %}

Bias in arrests – the entry point – is carried through the rest of the system.

{% include system_disparity_chart.html %}

A criminal record makes it more difficult to get an education, find housing, and go to work.

Chicago Public Schools students who are arrested are less likely to graduate high school and to attend a four-year college than their peers.

{% include education_outcomes_chart.html %}

SOURCE: Juvenile Arrest and Collateral Educational Damage in the Transition to Adulthood, a long-term study that tracked peer groups of CPS students across multiple years

58% of teens released from incarceration in Illinois return to prison within three years.

This is called recidivism, and it's a result of these obstacles combined with strict conditions of probation and parole.

It doesn't have to be this way.
