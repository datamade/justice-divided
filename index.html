Most teens break the law. Youth of color are more likely to be punished for it.

This disparity is called disproportionate minority contact. It has been recognized for decades as a deep-rooted problem of the juvenile justice system.

Researchers say the divide stems from bias in police and courts. It matters because contact with the justice system puts youth at a persistent disadvantage, affecting their ability to get an education, find housing, and go to work.

It's #NotJusticeWhen the system harms one group significantly more than another.

In Chicago, a disproportionate number of black youth are arrested.

How disproportionate?

The multiplier is equal to the probability a black youth is arrested, divided by the probability any youth is arrested.

As a result, the majority of youth at every stage of the juvenile justice system are black and brown.

An arrest alone can have lifelong consequences.

Chicago Public Schools students who are arrested are more likely to drop out of high school and less likely to attend a four-year college, compared to their peers.


SOURCE: Juvenile Arrest and Collateral Educational Damage in the Transition to Adulthood, a long-term study that tracked peer groups of CPS students across multiple years
