Most juveniles engage in “delinquent” behavior during their adolescence. Youth of color are more likely to become entangled in the juvenile justice system.

This phenomenon is known as “disproportionate minority contact.”

By way of example, meet two groups of teen boys. (n = 3250)

Group A
Group B

An equal number of boys from both groups use marijuana. (n = 650)

Group A
Group B

Fifteen boys from Group A are arrested on minor marijuana charges. Only one is arrested from Group B.

Group A
Group B

The difference?

Group A is black.

In Chicago, black youth are overrepresented among juvenile arrests.

As a consequence, they represent a wide majority at every subsequent stage of the justice system.

Youth who are arrested are more likely than their peers to drop out of high school.

Each represents a student. Students who are arrested have a 73% chance of dropping out. Their peers have a 51% chance of dropping out.

Fewer still enroll in four-year colleges.

Youth who are arrested have an 18% chance of attending a four-year college. Their peers have a 34% chance.

For most incarcerated youth, the justice system becomes a vicious cycle.

58% of youth released from the Illinois Department of Juvenile Justice return to prison within three years.
