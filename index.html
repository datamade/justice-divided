Most teens break the law. Youth of color are more likely to be punished for it.

This disparity is called disproportionate minority contact. It has been recognized for decades as a deep-rooted problem of the juvenile justice system.

Researchers say the divide stems from bias in police and courts. It matters because contact with the justice system puts youth at a persistent disadvantage.

It's #NotJusticeWhen the system unduly harms one group over another.

By way of example, consider two groups of teenaged boys in Chicago. Group A is black. Group B is white. (n = 3250)

Research shows an equal number of boys from both groups, about 20%, use marijuana. (n = 650)

Some boys are caught with a small amount of marijuana. At Chicago's current juvenile arrest rate, 16 boys are arrested. Only one of them is white.

Black and white youth account for similar proportions of the Chicago's juvenile population. Yet three times as many black youth are arrested for minor marijuana offenses.

Overall, black youth account for 26 times as many juvenile arrests as white youth.

As a consequence, they represent a wide majority at every subsequent stage of the justice system.

Youth who are arrested are more likely than their peers to drop out of high school.

Students who are arrested are twice as likely than their peers to drop out of high school, and half as likely to attend a four-year college.
