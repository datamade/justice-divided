Youth of all races break the law. Youth of color are more likely to be punished.


{% include system_disparity_chart.html %}

This inequality is called disproportionate minority contact (DMC for short), and it means the juvenile justice system impacts black and brown youth more than their white peers. Here's how.

Black youth are arrested more often, even for the same behavior.


For example, white, black, and Hispanic 12th graders self-report monthly marijuana use at the same rate, about one in five.













Source: “Monitoring the Future,” University of Michigan

Yet in 2014, black youth in Chicago were 1.6 times more likely to be arrested for it.

{% include expectation_v_reality_chart.html %}

This pattern is not only true of arrests overall – it's worse. In 2014, black youth in Chicago were 1.9 times more likely to be arrested than all youth in Chicago.

{% include arrest_v_population_chart.html %}

{% include district_map.html %}

Fewer arrests does not mean less bias.

Due to decriminalization, the number of arrests for marijuana possession has fallen to virtually zero in 2017.

{% include arrest_chart.html %}

While possession was still an arrestable offense, however, the proportion of arrests happening to black youths stayed the same.

{% include race_chart.html %}

A criminal record makes it more difficult to get an education, find housing, and go to work.

Chicago Public Schools students who are arrested are less likely to graduate high school and to attend a four-year college than their peers.

{% include education_outcomes_chart.html %}

58% of teens released from incarceration in Illinois return to prison within three years.

This is called recidivism, and it's a result of these obstacles combined with strict conditions of probation and parole.

It doesn't have to be this way.
