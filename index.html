Youth of all races break the law. Youth of color are more likely to be punished.


This inequality is called disproportionate minority contact (DMC for short), and it means black Chicago youth are impacted by the justice system more than their peers.

{% include system_disparity_chart.html %}

Black youth are arrested more often, even for the same behavior.


For example, one in five high school seniors say they've used marijuana in the last month. This is true of white, black, and Hispanic youth.

Source: “Monitoring the Future,” University of Michigan

If Chicago youth were treated the same, the youth who were arrested would look like the population.

{% include expected_arrests_chart.html %}

But they don't.

{% include actual_arrests_chart.html %}

This pattern is worse when you look at arrests for all offenses. In 2014, black youth in Chicago were 1.9 times more likely to be arrested than all youth in Chicago.

{% include arrest_v_population_chart.html %}

{% include district_map.html %}

Fewer arrests does not make arrests more fair.

Due to decriminalization, the number of arrests for marijuana possession has fallen over time.

{% include arrest_chart.html %}

As the number of arrests fell, the proportion of arrests of black youth stayed the same.

{% include race_chart.html %}

A criminal record makes it more difficult to get an education, find housing, and go to work.

Chicago Public Schools students who are arrested are less likely to graduate high school and to attend a four-year college than their peers.

Source: “Juvenile Arrest and Collateral Educational Damage in the Transition to Adulthood,” Sociology of Education

58% of teens released from incarceration in Illinois return to prison within three years.

This is called recidivism, and it's a result of these obstacles combined with strict conditions of probation and parole.

It doesn't have to be this way.
